What Is The Difference Between A Remaster And A Remake?

Many visitors to this site might already know the differences but it would be unfair to leave anyone in the dark on this matter. This will also hopefully show the purpose of this website so let us all be on the same page. I will lay out a small table of differences that will aid us along this explanation.

Remaster Remake
Improves visual fidelity, audio, and performance while keeping core gameplay. Rebuilds entire game from ground up, often using new engine, assets, and gameplay.
Updated to accommodate newer consoles to make it work. Overhauled to be native to new consoles, no revision to original code.
Usually marketed as an enhanced, polished version. Usually marketed as a modern, new take on an original.
Examples: Dark Souls Remastered, Last of Us Remastered Examples: Resident Evil 4(2023), Silent Hill 2 (2024)

Don't click off yet! It's still worth exploring further how these relate to the original release. These concepts existed even back then, when classic arcade games were remade to work on consoles (usually downgraded), but the first notable Remake that gains a reptutation as a remake is Super Mario All Stars, which remade the NES Mario Games for the SNES, including updated graphics and slight mechanical gameplay differences among other things. What really sets the release apart is that the jump from 8 bit to 16 bit graphics was incredible, and soon the craze about updating old games onto new consoles gained traction. But at the same time, there was talk about preserving these older games in their (mostly) original form, and so soon remasters and remakes gained their own seperate territory, but mostly, it was still about preserving older games in newer eras.

Remasters are the way they are because newer hardware allowed/required it and developers felt they could give an enhanced experience so the game could fit modern standards, which can be as simple as making the game go from 4:3 to 16:9 HD. Sometimes they even add or restore certain features, making it a sort of 'definitive' edition.

Remakes also came about due to newer or different hardware, and it had a similar goal to remasters, but while they often came out of neccessity, developers started to feel they could improve or give new visions to older games. Remakes allowed developers to reinvent their games in their full original vision that the newer hardware allowed. Thus out of this also came the want to make different visions come to life too, and from there...well capitalism commodifies art, so what better way to make money than remake a classic? Remasters are also subject to this, hence.. some pretty bad rereleases can come about from this.

But why would I make this site if these releases were the end all be all? They aren't, and they can vary considerably from the originals vision when making revisions. An artistic vision can be totally butchered in the process, and whether it's updating the code or making it from scratch, these versions develop their own distinct identity. It is worth informing gamers on how this can be, and which games this applies to. Why not read more articles to delve deeper into these issues? *wink wink*
